\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

\subsection{Datasets and splits}
\label{sec:exp:data}
We use a bilingual Mongolian--Chinese corpus consisting of two domains.
The \textbf{handwriting} domain ($H$) comprises $1\,003$ scanned archival
pages with $20\,115$ general-polygon text annotations (mean $20.1$
polygons per page, $\geq 4$ vertices per polygon). The \textbf{scene}
domain ($S$) comprises $1\,502$ natural-scene photographs with
$18\,598$ quadrilateral annotations (mean $12.4$ per image; $99.99\%$
are exact quadrilaterals, with a single five-vertex annotation that we
treat as a generic polygon). All transcription fields are placeholders
(\texttt{\#} for scene, \texttt{0} for handwriting), so we evaluate
detection only, without any recognition supervision. The handwriting
corpus contains one image that was unreferenced by any provided split;
we discard it for consistency.

We split each domain into train/val/test in a $70/15/15$ ratio,
producing $702/150/151$ for $H$ and $1\,051/225/226$ for $S$. All
splits are stored as MMOCR-style JSONL files alongside the code release.

\subsection{Implementation details}
\label{sec:exp:impl}
All experiments run on a single Apple M5 Pro device using the PyTorch
nightly MPS backend (no CUDA available); MPS-incompatible operators
(notably deformable convolutions) are explicitly avoided. The
backbone is ResNet-18 initialised from ImageNet weights via the
\texttt{torchvision} reference. Inputs are resized to $640 \times 640$;
training augmentation includes random scaling in $[0.75, 1.5]$, random
horizontal flip, and a colour-jitter perturbation. We optimise with
AdamW (lr $1\!\times\!10^{-4}$, weight decay $0.01$) and a batch size
of $4$ per domain (so each UDA step sees $4$ source + $4$ target
images). Each UDA training run uses $5$ epochs unless otherwise
noted, with a $3$-epoch pseudo-label warm-up; per-epoch walltime is
$\sim$5--10 minutes, total $\sim$30--60 minutes per run.

For post-processing we report two configurations: a \emph{fixed}
configuration with binarisation threshold \texttt{box-thresh}$=0.45$
and instance-keep threshold \texttt{min-score}$=0.5$ (the values used
during validation throughout training, so the per-epoch
\texttt{best\_student} selection is consistent), and a \emph{swept}
configuration that performs a $30$-combination grid search over
$\{0.3, 0.35, 0.4, 0.45, 0.5\} \times \{0.4, 0.45, 0.5, 0.55, 0.6, 0.65\}$
on the target test set and reports the highest-$\hmean$ combination
per checkpoint. The same swept protocol is applied to source-only
baselines for a fair head-to-head comparison.

\subsection{Cross-domain motivation: oracle vs.\ source-only}
\label{sec:exp:motivation}

Table~\ref{tab:motivation} establishes the cross-domain gap that
motivates this work. Each domain's oracle (trained and evaluated
in-domain) achieves a strong $\hmean$; on the cross-domain task,
performance collapses by one to two orders of magnitude. The
$\stoh$ source-only column is exactly zero: not a small number,
but no true positives at all on any of the $151$ handwriting test
images, at any post-process threshold we tried. This zero-floor
condition is the operative definition of cold start; it is also the
configuration where the cold-start fix of Sec.~\ref{sec:method:coldstart}
delivers the largest payoff.

\subsection{Asymmetric UDA gain analysis}
\label{sec:exp:asym}
The fixed-threshold column of Table~\ref{tab:main} alone might suggest
UDA delivers a roughly uniform $+2\!\sim\!3$~pp gain on both directions.
The swept-threshold column tells a different story. After both
source-only and UDA models are post-processed at their respective best
threshold combinations, the $\hsource$ gap shrinks to $+0.14$~pp -- which
we have empirically estimated to be within MPS run-to-run variance from
direct re-runs of v3 ($0.0596$ vs.\ $0.0633$, a $0.4$~pp swing on the
validation split). Threshold sweeping recovers most of what the
self-training pipeline appeared to contribute. By contrast, the
$\stoh$ swept gap \emph{widens} relative to the fixed gap, from
$+2.85$~pp to $+6.26$~pp: the UDA checkpoint actually benefits from
\emph{looser} thresholds (best at $0.35/0.45$) while the source-only
model has nothing meaningful to extract from any threshold.

We interpret this asymmetry as a regime distinction. In the $\hsource$
regime, the source-only model already encodes generic text-edge
features that activate on the target domain; the question is one of
calibration, not capability, and threshold tuning suffices. In the
$\stoh$ regime, no amount of post-processing can recover detections
from a model that produces no above-threshold response anywhere on the
target image -- UDA must \emph{create} the capability, which in turn
demands non-trivial pseudo-labels, which in turn demand the cold-start
fix. The headline finding is therefore that self-training UDA is most
useful precisely where source-only is most broken, and contributes
little above careful post-processing where source-only is already
weakly capable.

\subsection{Long-training analysis}
\label{sec:exp:long}
To check whether the $5$-epoch budget under-trains the model, we
extended both directions to $25$ epochs at the otherwise identical v3
configuration. Fig.~\ref{fig:longrun} shows the per-epoch target
validation $\hmean$ for both directions.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/long_run_curves.pdf}
  \caption{Long-training curves over $25$ epochs at $w_p^{\max}=0.1$.
  $\hsource$ peaks at epoch~$3$ ($\hmean=0.0596$) and degrades to
  $\sim$$0.005$ by epoch~$18$; $\stoh$ peaks at epoch~$1$
  ($\hmean=0.0353$) and collapses to zero by epoch~$8$, remaining at
  zero through the end of training. Both directions exhibit the same
  teacher--student drift collapse mechanism, with $\stoh$ exhibiting a
  much narrower stable window.}
  \label{fig:longrun}
\end{figure}

Three observations support a structural -- rather than budget --
interpretation of the $5$-epoch sweet spot. First, the long-run best
$\hmean$ on $\hsource$ ($0.0596$ at ep~$3$) is statistically
indistinguishable from the $5$-epoch best ($0.0633$); longer training
does not unlock additional gain. Second, after the peak, precision
trends upward as recall collapses -- the network becomes monotonically
more conservative under the influence of an increasingly cautious EMA
teacher. Third, the conservative $w_p^{\max}=0.1$ is sufficient to
trigger collapse on both directions; aggressive weights only make the
collapse faster. The empirical conclusion is that the $5$-epoch budget
is the stable regime for our pipeline, not a compromise imposed by
compute.

\subsection{Hyperparameter ablation}
\label{sec:exp:ablation}
Table~\ref{tab:ablation} compares three pseudo-label
configurations on the $\hsource$ direction, holding all other
hyperparameters constant. v1 (literature default,
$w_p^{\max}=0.5$, $\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.55$) matches v3's epoch-3
$\hmean$ but degrades much faster: by epoch~5 the
v1 pseudo-loss has saturated and target $\hmean$ has dropped to
$0.031$, below the source-only baseline. v2 raises
$\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}$ to $0.7$, which silences the teacher entirely
by epoch~5 (pseudo coverage falls to $0$); the model relies on
source-only supervision plus DANN and never benefits from the
self-training signal. v3 ($w_p^{\max}=0.1$, $\tau_{\mathrm{conf}}=0.55$)
matches the peak of v1 while keeping pseudo coverage intact and
collapsing more gracefully. We adopt v3 as the default UDA
configuration in all main-table numbers.
